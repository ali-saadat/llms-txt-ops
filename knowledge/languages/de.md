# Deutsch (de) — German

German business communication conventions. Notable for systematic use of formal `Sie` form and `Herr`/`Frau` honorifics. Direct communication style is common; not rude.

## Formality calibration

| Context | Formality | Form of address |
|---|---|---|
| Close colleagues, same level (Du-Kultur) | Casual: "Hallo [Vorname]," | Informal `du` |
| Cross-functional, established thread | Professional: "Hallo [Vorname]," | Formal `Sie` (default in business) |
| Senior colleagues / management | "Sehr geehrte/r [Frau/Herr] [Nachname]," | Formal `Sie` throughout |
| External / vendor | "Sehr geehrte/r Frau/Herr [Nachname]," | Fully formal `Sie` |
| Tech startup context | Often `du` from the start | Du-Kultur is common in tech |
| Traditional corporate | Almost always `Sie` | Default to formal |

## Honorifics

- **Herr** — Mr.
- **Frau** — Mrs./Ms. (Fräulein is archaic; never use)
- **Sehr geehrte/r** — "Most esteemed" — gendered: `geehrter Herr X` / `geehrte Frau X`
- **Dr.** — academic title; **always use** if person has a doctorate (very important in Germany)
- **Prof.** — professor; use with academic affiliations
- Multiple academic titles stack: `Sehr geehrter Herr Prof. Dr. Schmidt`

Critical: **Germans take academic titles seriously**. If someone has a Dr. or Prof. title in their email signature, use it. Failing to use it is a real misstep.

## Formal Sie vs informal du

In business email, `Sie` is default. Wait for the other party to offer `du` ("Wollen wir uns nicht duzen?") before switching. Premature switching to `du` is awkward.

In tech startups (especially Berlin, Munich), `du` is often the default from the start — but cross-company communication usually starts with `Sie`.

| Informal `du` | Formal `Sie` | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `du hast geschickt` | `Sie haben geschickt` | you sent |
| `dein Vorschlag` | `Ihr Vorschlag` | your suggestion |
| `kannst du` | `können Sie` | can you |
| `dich` | `Sie` | you (accusative) |

## Common business email phrases

| Purpose | Deutsch |
|---|---|
| Opening thanks | "Vielen Dank für die sorgfältige Überprüfung und das ausführliche Feedback" |
| Acknowledging concern | "Ich möchte dies direkt ansprechen" |
| Presenting findings | "Die Ergebnisse unserer Überprüfung..." |
| Honest framing | "Ich möchte hier transparent sein, was Erfolg in diesem Kontext bedeutet" |
| Citing empirical evidence | "Gemäß der SE Ranking-Studie (n=300.000 Domains, November 2025)..." |
| Closing with offer | "Bei Fragen stehe ich Ihnen gerne zur Verfügung" |
| Polite disagreement | "Ich sehe das etwas anders, weil..." |

## Closing patterns

| Formality | Closing |
|---|---|
| Standard formal | "Mit freundlichen Grüßen," |
| Slightly warmer | "Beste Grüße," |
| Most formal | "Hochachtungsvoll," (rare in modern business) |
| Casual professional | "Viele Grüße," / "Schöne Grüße," |
| Du-context | "Liebe Grüße," |
| Acronym shortcut (informal) | "MfG," (Mit freundlichen Grüßen) — but spell out in real emails |

## Three-reasons framing

> "Drei Gründe, die einer kritischen Prüfung standhalten: (1) eine kaputte bestehende Datei zu ersetzen ist eindeutig besser, (2) die Datei dient gleichzeitig als interne Grundlage für unsere eigene KI-Arbeit, (3) Forward-Kompatibilität kostet uns nichts. Der Fall ist defensiv, nicht offensiv."

## Date and number formats

- Date: `15.05.2026` (DD.MM.YYYY)
- Number: `1.234,56` (period as thousands, comma as decimal)
- Currency: `1.234,56 €` or `EUR 1.234,56`
- Time: 24-hour clock standard (`14:30 Uhr`)

## Cultural notes

- **Directness is valued**, not rude. Germans appreciate clear, concrete communication.
- **Concise emails are appreciated** — avoid US-style "Hope you're doing well!" openings.
- **Salutation matches closing tone** — if you opened with "Sehr geehrte/r" you close with "Mit freundlichen Grüßen". If you opened with "Hallo" you can close with "Viele Grüße".
- **GDPR awareness is high** — German stakeholders particularly attentive to privacy implications of any data-handling discussion.
- **Punctuality is expected** in meetings; mention specific times when scheduling.

## German-specific llms.txt concerns

- GDPR is the primary regulatory framework — multilingual sites serving German users must consider data minimization and storage limitation
- Cookie banners (TTDSG) are mandatory and well-enforced
- Many German business sites have an "Impressum" (legal notice) page that's mandatory — include in llms.txt as canonical authoritative content

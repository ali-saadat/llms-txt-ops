# 한국어 (ko) — Korean

Korean business communication conventions. Korean has formal speech levels (존댓말 / jondaetmal) and hierarchical sensitivity. Getting tone right is critical for business relationships.

## Formality is critical

Korean has multiple speech levels:
- **하십시오체** (hasipsio-che) — formal/polite, used in business writing, presentations
- **해요체** (haeyo-che) — polite informal, common in spoken business
- **반말** (banmal) — casual, only with close peers or juniors

**Business email defaults to 하십시오체 (formal).** Even casual workplace emails preserve the polite ending.

## Honorific titles

Korean business uses title + name + honorific suffix:

- **님 (-nim)** — universal respectful suffix, attaches after titles or names
- **씨 (-ssi)** — added to names for peer-level respect (like "Mr./Ms." but more personal)
- **선생님 (sŏnsaengnim)** — "teacher" — for educators, doctors, lawyers
- **이사 (isa)** — director
- **상무 (sangmu)** — managing director
- **팀장 (timjang)** — team leader
- **부장 (bujang)** — department head
- **대표 (daepyo)** — representative / CEO

**Title is used WITHOUT last name in address.** E.g., "Kim 부장님" (Director Kim) or just "부장님" (Director).

In email subject/opening, use 직급님 (title + 님) or 직급 + 성함 (title + full name).

## Korean name conventions

- Korean names are written family-name-first: `김민지` (Kim Minji)
- In English contexts, often reversed: "Minji Kim"
- Use the order the person uses in their own signature
- Don't address by given name alone in business; use full name + title or just title + 님

## Common business email structure

```
[Recipient title + 님 OR 회사명 + 직급님]

안녕하십니까. (annyŏng-hasimnikka — formal greeting)
[Your company]의 [your name]입니다.

[Body — concise, organized, often with numbered points]

감사합니다. (kamsahamnida — "thank you")

[Your name]
[Your company]
[Your contact info]
```

## Common business email phrases

| Purpose | 한국어 (Romanization) |
|---|---|
| Standard opening | 안녕하십니까 (annyŏng-hasimnikka) — formal "hello" |
| Self-introduction | [회사명]의 [이름]입니다 — "I'm [name] from [company]" |
| Opening thanks | 꼼꼼히 검토해 주셔서 진심으로 감사드립니다 (kkomkkomhi gŏmto-hae jusyŏsŏ jinsim-eu-ro kamsa-deuri-mnida) — "thank you sincerely for your thorough review" |
| Apologetic | 번거롭게 해드려 죄송합니다 (bŏngŏ-rop-ge hae-deu-ryŏ joesong-hamnida) — "sorry for the trouble" |
| Presenting findings | 확인 결과를 보고드립니다 (hwakin gyŏlgwa-reul bogo-deuri-mnida) — "I report the verification results" |
| Polite request | 부탁드립니다 (butak-deuri-mnida) — formal "please" |
| Acknowledging mistake | 죄송합니다 (joesong-hamnida) — "I apologize" |
| Closing courtesy | 잘 부탁드립니다 (jal butak-deuri-mnida) — "please take care of this" |

## Closing patterns

| Context | Closing |
|---|---|
| Standard formal | 감사합니다 (kamsahamnida — "thank you") |
| More formal | 감사드립니다 (kamsa-deuri-mnida) |
| Following request | 잘 부탁드립니다 (jal butak-deuri-mnida) |
| Following thank-you | 좋은 하루 보내십시오 (joh-eun haru bonae-sipsio — "have a good day") |

After the closing phrase, name + company + title + contact info.

## Date and number formats

- Date: `2026년 5월 15일` (formal) or `2026-05-15` (technical)
- Number: `1,234.56` (Western format)
- Currency: `₩1,234` or `1,234원`
- Time: 24-hour clock or 12-hour with 오전/오후 (AM/PM)

## Cultural notes

- **Hierarchical sensitivity** — copy senior colleagues even on minor matters
- **Indirect communication for difficult topics** — direct refusal is uncommon
- **Face (체면 / chemyeon)** — avoid embarrassing anyone in writing
- **KakaoTalk dominance** — much Korean business communication happens on KakaoTalk rather than email
- **English mixing** — common in Korean tech: "OK", "review", "스케줄" (schedule), etc.
- **Holiday awareness** — Lunar New Year (설날), Chuseok (추석), affect availability
- **PIPA (Personal Information Protection Act)** — Korea's GDPR equivalent

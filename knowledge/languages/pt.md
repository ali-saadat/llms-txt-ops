# Português (pt) — Portuguese

Portuguese business communication conventions. Significant variation between Brazilian Portuguese (pt-BR) and European Portuguese (pt-PT). African Portuguese (Angola, Mozambique) generally follows European conventions but with some regional vocabulary.

## Variant matters

- **Brazilian Portuguese (pt-BR)**: Larger market, generally more direct/casual in business
- **European Portuguese (pt-PT)**: More formal, more European-style conventions
- **African Portuguese**: Follows European with regional variation

Match the recipient's variant. Don't mix.

## Formality calibration

| Context | Formality |
|---|---|
| Close colleagues (Brazil) | Casual: "Oi [Nome]," | Informal `você` |
| Close colleagues (Portugal) | "Olá [Nome]," | Formal `você` or informal `tu` |
| Cross-functional thread | "Olá [Nome]," | Formal `você` (Brazil), `senhor/senhora` (Portugal) |
| Senior / management | "Prezado/a [Nome]," (formal) | Fully formal |
| External / vendor | "Prezado/a Sr./Sra. [Sobrenome]," | Fully formal |

## Honorifics

- **Sr.** (Senhor) — Mr.
- **Sra.** (Senhora) — Mrs./Ms.
- **Prezado/a** — "Esteemed" (gendered)
- **Dr./Dra.** — Doctor (academic; commonly used as a courtesy in Brazil for lawyers and others with university degrees)
- **Engenheiro/a** — Engineer; used in Brazilian and European business as title (with last name)

Brazilian convention extends "Doctor" usage broadly — anyone with a university degree might be addressed as "Doctor" as a courtesy. European Portuguese is more reserved.

## Forms of address

In Brazil:
- **você** — universal "you" (formal AND informal)
- **o senhor / a senhora** — extra formal

In Portugal:
- **tu** — informal "you" (peers, friends)
- **você** — formal "you" (still used as a polite form)
- **o senhor / a senhora** — very formal

## Honest-expectations opening (translated, Brazilian)

> "Antes de prosseguirmos, gostaria de definir expectativas realistas sobre o que o llms.txt fará e não fará — porque três estudos independentes (SE Ranking com 300 mil domínios, OtterlyAI com 90 dias de logs, Search Engine Land com teste de 10 sites) não encontraram nenhum aumento mensurável em citações de IA."

## Three-reasons framing (translated)

> "Três razões que resistem ao escrutínio: (1) substituir um arquivo existente quebrado é uma melhoria clara, (2) o arquivo serve também como base interna para nosso próprio trabalho de IA, (3) compatibilidade futura não tem custo. O argumento é defensivo, não ofensivo."

## Date and number formats

- Date Brazil: `15/05/2026` (DD/MM/YYYY) or `15 de maio de 2026` (formal)
- Date Portugal: same as Brazil
- Number Brazil: `1.234,56` (period as thousands, comma as decimal)
- Number Portugal: `1 234,56` (space as thousands)
- Currency Brazil: `R$ 1.234,56`
- Currency Portugal: `1.234,56 €` or `€ 1.234,56`

## Cultural notes

- **Brazilian directness**: more conversational in tech business; "Beleza?" or "Tudo bem?" common openers
- **Portuguese formality**: somewhat more formal; traditional business letter structure preserved
- **Lusophone Africa**: business culture varies — Angola tends more traditional, Mozambique somewhat similar
- **Time-zone awareness**: Brazil spans 4 time zones; Portugal aligned with UK
- **Mixing English**: common in Brazilian tech; less common in European Portuguese
- **GDPR / LGPD (Brazil)**: Brazil has LGPD modeled on GDPR; both Brazilian and European Portuguese stakeholders attentive to privacy
- **Carnival / Fado / cultural references**: avoid in formal business unless contextually appropriate

## Portuguese-specific llms.txt concerns

- **LGPD (Brazil's GDPR)** — Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados
- **GDPR for Portugal**
- **Multi-language ecommerce** — Brazilian retailers often serve Portuguese + Spanish-speaking customers; per-locale llms.txt
- **Diacritics in URLs** — Portuguese URLs often contain accented characters (`á`, `ã`, `ç`, etc.). Verify UTF-8 handling.

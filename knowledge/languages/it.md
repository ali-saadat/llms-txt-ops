# Italiano (it) ‚Äî Italian

Italian business communication conventions. Italian business culture values warmth and personal connection alongside professional formality.

## Formality calibration

| Context | Formality | Form of address |
|---|---|---|
| Close colleagues | Casual: "Ciao [Nome]," | Informal `tu` |
| Cross-functional, ongoing thread | Professional: "Buongiorno [Nome]," | Formal `Lei` (third-person singular) |
| Senior / management | "Gentile [Sig./Sig.ra] [Cognome]," | Fully formal `Lei` |
| External / vendor | "Egregio/a Sig./Sig.ra [Cognome]," | Fully formal |
| Northern Italy business (Milan, Turin) | Generally more direct, faster `tu` transition | Often `tu` after a few exchanges |
| Southern Italy business | Generally more formal initially | Stays with `Lei` longer |

## Honorifics

- **Sig.** (Signore) ‚Äî Mr.
- **Sig.ra** (Signora) ‚Äî Mrs./Ms.
- **Sig.na** (Signorina) ‚Äî Miss (rarely used in modern business)
- **Dott./Dott.ssa** ‚Äî Doctor (academic title; common in Italian business as a courtesy for university graduates)
- **Ing.** ‚Äî Engineer (used as title with last name)
- **Avv.** ‚Äî Avvocato (Lawyer; used as title)
- **Prof.** ‚Äî Professor

Italian business culture takes academic titles seriously. If someone is "Dott. Rossi" in their signature, use it.

## Formal "Lei" vs informal "tu"

Default to `Lei` (third-person formal) in business. The transition to `tu` ("ci diamo del tu?") usually comes from the senior party.

| Informal `tu` | Formal `Lei` | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `hai visto` | `ha visto` | you saw |

| `il tuo` | `il Suo` (capitalized) | your |
